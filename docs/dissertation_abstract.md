# Dissertation Abstract

**The Effects of the Proportion Contributed to Household Income on Satisfaction with Total Household Income Amongst UK Spouses with Children Under the Age of 16 — A Quantitative Study**

*Evangelos Pourikas*
*City, St George's, University of London — BSc Criminology with Data Analytics*
*May 2025 | Grade: 75% (First-Class Honours)*

---

The effects of the proportion that an individual contributes to their respective household income on their satisfaction with it amongst couples with young children is a complex relationship which lacks scrutiny in the United Kingdom. In this paper, I explored these effects with the utilisation of relevant knowledge around this domain and quantitative methods. I made use of Wave 14 of the Understanding Society dataset and conducted methodologically sound bivariate and logistic regression analyses.

With the use of these resources, I found that the relationship that was being examined proved to be representative of the population and the people whose satisfaction with their household income was negatively affected the least were those who do not contribute anything and those who contribute everything to the total household income, demonstrating that households which adopt a dual-earner model show less satisfaction.

Furthermore, I find that mothers are more likely to report dissatisfaction with their household income when they contribute more than their spouse, whereas fathers show the opposite. However, overall, fathers are found as more likely to be dissatisfied with their household income than women.

Importantly, a predictor that was tested to evaluate its influence on satisfaction with household income and proved to be highly significant was general health. This analysis offers a comprehensive examination of the effects of household income contribution on satisfaction with household income (amongst UK spouses with children under the age of 16), while quantitatively accounting for some significant factors (like general health) and theoretically acknowledging others (i.e. gender breadwinning norms, etc.).
